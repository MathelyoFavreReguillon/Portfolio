DfAM & Topology Optimization

Literature review and engineering study on the integration of topology optimization into Design for Additive Manufacturing workflows, with a focus on software tools, manufacturing constraints, additive processes and a case study based on the EPFL Racing Team upright.

Project Overview

This project explores how additive manufacturing changes the way parts should be designed, and how topology optimization can be used to generate lightweight, high-performance geometries adapted to these new manufacturing freedoms.

The work was structured as a literature review supported by a full presentation and a written report, covering Design for Additive Manufacturing principles, optimization methods, production technologies, industrial limits and a practical case study linked to EPFL Racing Team.

Key Contributions

  • Research and synthesis on the relationship between DfAM and topology optimization
  • Study of optimization workflows, structural constraints and manufacturability limits
  • Comparison of software tools such as Altair Inspire, nTopology, Fusion, Siemens NX, Ansys and 3DEXPERIENCE
  • Review of additive manufacturing processes for topology-optimized parts
  • Contribution to the EPFL Racing Team upright case study and technical communication material

Design for Additive Manufacturing

A central idea of the project is that additive manufacturing does not remove constraints, but transforms them. Designing for AM therefore means using geometric freedom, functional integration and lightweight strategies while also accounting for support generation, build orientation, anisotropy and post-processing.

The study highlights the transition from simple part replacement to real AM-oriented redesign, where components can be rethought to reduce mass, integrate functions and improve overall performance.

Topology Optimization Principles

Topology optimization is presented as a computational method that searches for the best material distribution inside a design space in order to maximize structural performance under defined constraints.

The project reviews major optimization families such as SIMP density-based methods, evolutionary methods like ESO/BESO, and the role of post-processing to convert raw optimization outputs into manufacturable geometries.

Rules & Constraints

  • Mechanical constraints: stiffness, displacement, stress and dynamic behaviour
  • Geometric constraints: minimum feature size, connectivity and printable thickness
  • Manufacturing limits: overhang angles, unsupported holes and powder removal
  • Build orientation effects on anisotropy, fatigue and support strategy
  • Need to integrate manufacturing constraints early to avoid impractical “optimal” shapes

Software & Workflow

A significant part of the work focused on the digital chain from optimization to fabrication: defining the design space, applying loads and boundary conditions, running topology optimization, reconstructing geometry, validating the part and preparing it for additive manufacturing.

The study compares how different software environments address this chain, from CAD integration and implicit modeling to simulation, printing constraints and preparation for industrial production.

Additive Manufacturing Processes

The project also reviews the main additive manufacturing technologies used for topology-optimized parts, from early-stage prototyping processes to structural metal production methods.

Particular attention is given to the trade-offs between resolution, mechanical performance, support requirements, surface quality, cost and suitability for complex optimized shapes, with laser powder bed fusion standing out as the main industrial solution for high-performance structural parts.

Advantages & Limitations

  • Mass reduction while maintaining stiffness and strength targets
  • Part consolidation and integration of internal or multifunctional features
  • Strong synergy with AM for organic and highly efficient geometries
  • Limitations linked to computation time, smoothing, post-processing and standards maturity
  • Real-world performance still depends on process effects such as anisotropy, residual stresses and manufacturing quality

EPFL Racing Team Case Study

The case study focuses on the upright developed for the EPFL Racing Team, a highly loaded mechanical component linking suspension elements, braking interfaces, gearbox integration and motor housing while also incorporating cooling functions.

This example shows why topology optimization is particularly relevant in motorsport: the upright is a structural and unsprung component, so reducing mass while maintaining stiffness directly contributes to vehicle performance.

Project Outcome

Beyond the technical content, this project demonstrates the full reasoning chain behind modern high-performance design: understanding AM-specific constraints, using topology optimization intelligently, selecting the right process and translating an optimized concept into a realistic engineering solution.

It also reflects a strong communication component through the production of a complete report and a scientific-style presentation synthesizing the main conclusions of the study.
