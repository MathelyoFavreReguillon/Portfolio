Development of High-Temperature High-Strength Aluminium Alloys

Computational study of an Al-Ce-Sc alloy system using Thermo-Calc to design aluminium alloys with improved thermal stability and strength for service temperatures well above conventional aluminium applications.

Project Overview

This project explored the design of new aluminium alloys capable of retaining mechanical performance at elevated temperature, where conventional high-strength aluminium alloys rapidly lose strength. The study focused on a cast Al-Ce-Sc system combining two complementary strengthening mechanisms.

Cerium was introduced to form the thermally stable Al₁₁Ce₃ eutectic, while scandium was used to generate coherent Al₃Sc precipitates in the α-Al matrix. The objective was to combine eutectic stability and precipitation strengthening in order to create a lightweight alloy with improved service capability in the 300–350 °C range.

Software Thermo-Calc used for phase stability, precipitation and diffusion analysis.
Alloy concept ~10 wt.% Ce for Al₁₁Ce₃ eutectic and 0.2–1 wt.% Sc for Al₃Sc precipitates.
Target range Designed for moderate high-temperature service around 300–350 °C.
Main objective Increase aluminium thermal stability while maintaining high strength.

Methodology

The work began with a thermodynamic study of the Al-Ce-Sc system to verify the stability of the expected phases and identify a relevant alloy design. Cerium was fixed around the eutectic composition, while scandium content was varied between 0.2, 0.5 and 1 wt.% in order to compare the effect of precipitate strengthening.

Separate computational workflows were then used for each mechanism: diffusion analysis for the Al₁₁Ce₃ eutectic phase, and precipitation plus coarsening analysis for Al₃Sc. The thermal stability of the system was evaluated through interfacial diffusion, precipitate growth and the evolution of yield strength over time.

Design Strategy

The alloy design was built around two distinct structural roles: a thermally stable eutectic to preserve high-temperature integrity, and nanoscale precipitates to strengthen the aluminium matrix and reduce strength mismatch between phases.

  • Al₁₁Ce₃ eutectic for thermal stability and elevated-temperature resistance
  • Al₃Sc precipitates for matrix strengthening and improved yield strength
  • Diffusion analysis to verify interface stability over time
  • Coarsening analysis to assess long-term service behaviour at 300 °C and 350 °C

Main Results

The simulations showed that the Al₁₁Ce₃ eutectic has extremely high thermal stability, with negligible interfacial diffusion over the investigated conditions. On the scandium side, Al₃Sc precipitates provided significant strengthening of the α-Al matrix, with computed yield strengths increasing strongly with scandium content.

From an engineering standpoint, the main interest of this alloy system lies in the combination of long-term eutectic stability and controllable matrix strengthening. This makes the Al-Ce-Sc concept particularly relevant for lightweight components operating beyond the typical thermal limits of standard aluminium alloys.

At 300 °C, the alloy system remains promising over long durations, with limited strength degradation after coarsening. At 350 °C, however, precipitate coarsening becomes much more severe and significantly reduces long-term strength retention. The study therefore highlights both the potential of the concept and the need for further optimization, especially regarding cost, heat treatment and experimental validation.

Eutectic stability Al₁₁Ce₃ shows almost no diffusion and remains highly stable at elevated temperature.
Matrix strengthening Al₃Sc precipitation raises yield strength from roughly 160 to 380 MPa depending on Sc content.
Temperature limit ~300 °C is promising for long-term use, while 350 °C becomes more critical due to coarsening.
