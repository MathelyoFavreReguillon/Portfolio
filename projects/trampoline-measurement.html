Dynamic Measurement on a Trampoline-Like Membrane

Experimental study of the dynamic interaction between a ball and a trampoline-like membrane, combining deformation tracking, rebound velocity measurement and acceleration analysis through a repeatable pendulum-based setup.

Project Overview

This project investigated the response of a trampoline-like membrane subjected to repeated impacts from a ball. The initial idea was to better understand how a flexible surface stores and returns energy during contact, in a simplified configuration inspired by trampoline dynamics.

Early free-fall experiments proved difficult to reproduce consistently, so the setup was redesigned around a guided pendulum system. This made it possible to control the impact conditions more precisely and to compare results at several release angles under repeatable experimental conditions.

Test principle A ball mounted on a pendulum impacts a vertically tensioned elastic membrane.
Control parameter Release angle α = 34°, 53°, and 70° used to vary impact severity.
Measurements Membrane deformation, rebound velocity and impact acceleration.
Main objective Build a reliable and repeatable dynamic measurement setup.

Methodology

The experiment combined three complementary measurement systems. First, the membrane deformation field was extracted from high-speed video using PIV analysis. Second, the ball’s rebound velocity was measured with two light-dependent resistors positioned near the membrane. Third, an onboard accelerometer was used to capture the acceleration profile during impact and rebound.

Several repetitions were performed for each measurement type in order to evaluate repeatability. The pendulum architecture, release stops and sensor arrangement were all designed to reduce variability and create consistent initial conditions across trials.

Measurement Systems

The project was structured around three different but connected measurement approaches, each targeting a different part of the dynamic response of the system.

  • PIV-based deformation field analysis of the membrane surface
  • LDR-based rebound velocity measurement after impact
  • Accelerometer-based measurement of rebound acceleration peaks
  • Repeated trials to assess precision and statistical consistency

Main Results

The membrane deformation increased clearly with the release angle, confirming that higher impact velocity leads to a stronger local response. The deformation fields remained centered and coherent across trials, indicating that the setup produced reproducible impacts and a consistent deformation pattern.

The rebound velocity and acceleration measurements followed the same global trend: stronger initial launches produced larger rebound velocities and higher first impact accelerations. Despite some limitations at the most severe impacts, the overall standard deviations remained low, supporting the reliability of the experimental protocol.

Membrane deformation Maximum displacement increases significantly from 34° to 70°, with low dispersion between trials.
Rebound velocity Mean first rebound velocity rises from about 0.77 m/s at 34° to about 1.82 m/s at 70°.
Acceleration Peak acceleration is highest at large angles, with overall repeatability improving after setup stabilization.

Engineering Interpretation

The project shows that the dynamic response of a flexible membrane can be characterized with a relatively simple laboratory setup, provided the boundary conditions and impact conditions are sufficiently controlled. The guided pendulum was a key design decision because it replaced poorly repeatable free-fall trials with a more stable and measurable input.

Beyond the physical results themselves, the project highlights the importance of sensor choice, acquisition rate and setup rigidity in experimental mechanics. The quality of the data depended as much on measurement design as on the underlying system dynamics.

Limits and Improvements

The report identifies several possible improvements to further refine the experiment. The main limitations came from mechanical losses in the pendulum, occasional obstruction effects near the light sensors, and setup motion during the strongest impacts before the structure was fully secured.

  • Slimmer pendulum arm to reduce optical interference
  • Lower-friction pivot with ball bearing for better repeatability
  • Improved membrane surface preparation for higher PIV quality
  • Further refinement of sensor integration and signal acquisition
