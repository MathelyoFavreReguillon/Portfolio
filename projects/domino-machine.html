Domino Placement Machine

Design and development of a manually actuated machine capable of placing dominos at a regular interval in straight lines and curves, while combining storage, steering and synchronized deposition mechanisms into one compact mechanical system.

Project Overview

This project was carried out as part of the Mechanical Construction course at EPFL and focused on the complete design of a domino placement machine developed over 14 weeks.

The goal was to create a machine able to place dominos one by one with a regular spacing, while remaining compact, manually operated, mechanically reliable and compatible with both straight and curved trajectories.

Key Contributions

  • Concept generation and comparison for storage, steering and deposition systems
  • Mechanical sizing of steering, gearing, deposition and loading mechanisms
  • Development of CAD models, kinematic studies and dimensioning calculations
  • Definition of a manufacturable architecture using standard machining processes
  • Contribution to full documentation including assembly logic and user operation

Initial Specifications

The machine had to store 50 dominos, place them vertically, maintain a spacing of 20 ± 1 mm, and work in straight lines as well as in fixed-radius and free steering modes.

Additional constraints included a maximum height of 300 mm, a mass below 3 kg, one-handed operation, easy reloading, and compatibility with a minimum free-turn radius of 200 mm.

Engineering Challenge

The core challenge was to combine three major functions into one coherent system: storage of the dominos, controlled steering of the machine, and precise deposition with regular spacing.

The final concept had to remain compact and manufacturable while avoiding blockage, preserving stability, and ensuring that placement accuracy was not affected by trajectory changes.

Chosen Concept

  • Two vertical magazines of 25 dominos each to satisfy the height constraint
  • Asynchronous feeding from both magazines to avoid manual changeover
  • Central drive wheel used to keep the deposition frequency independent from turning
  • Gear-driven transmission with a 2.5 ratio between the machine wheel and the deposition arms
  • Rack-based steering allowing both fixed and free trajectory modes

Mechanical Architecture

Dominos are stored vertically, pushed alternately by a projection mechanism, guided through curved ramps that bring them from horizontal to vertical, and finally centered by a dedicated deposition system.

The design combines bevel and spur gears, linkage systems, racks, sliding parts and dedicated supports in order to synchronize motion transfer from the rolling machine to the domino output system.

Design Development

Several alternatives were explored during the project, including inclined magazines, barrel-like storage concepts, belt transmission solutions, single-wheel steering concepts and different domino centering strategies.

The final solution was selected because it offered the best compromise between reliability, manufacturability, compactness, loading simplicity and respect of the technical specifications.

Calculations & Validation

  • Manual tests and benchmark study of an existing commercial domino machine
  • Kinematic sizing of the steering and fixed-radius modes
  • Transmission ratio fixed at 2.5 to match deposition frequency requirements
  • Maximum machine speed estimated at 2.8 cm/s for controlled placement
  • Final overall dimensions about 224 × 92 × 288 mm

Manufacturing Perspective

A strong part of the project consisted in adapting the design to realistic machining constraints, with most parts designed for turning or 3-axis milling.

Material choices included PA6 for many structural parts, PTFE for low-friction sliding elements, aluminum for shafts, rubber for wheel grip and titanium for a magazine reloading component requiring flexibility and durability.

Project Outcome

The final machine satisfies the project requirements while demonstrating a complete engineering workflow: concept generation, mechanical reasoning, CAD design, dimensioning, manufacturability analysis and user-oriented operation logic.

Beyond the mechanism itself, the project was also a strong team-based design exercise involving planning, task distribution, iterative problem solving and technical communication through a full engineering report.
